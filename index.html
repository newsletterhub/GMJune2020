Where does the NYC market go from here?
I want to wish everyone a happy start to the summer season! Finally, we've reached the opening of New York City for business, including the real estate market!
 
The New York State-mandated PAUSE order, which commenced on March 22 and prohibited in-person showings, brought the local residential real estate market to a near standstill for 90 days. As a result, new listings and signed contracts took a nosedive, down about 80 percent from 2019. Most contract activity reflected deals where buyers had viewed properties before the shutdown.
 
Per market sources, a majority of the contracts signed were in the below $2 million price segment, including many studios and 1BD/1BA apartments. In several cases, deals were renegotiated with further price discounts on average of 5-7 percent to compensate buyers for future market uncertainty and the potential for a recession. Activity in the luxury market ($4 million and above) halted except for a few contracts per week (versus last spring's market where 20 deals signed each week, on average).
 
Where do we go from here? It's likely to take several months for the market to recalibrate for some degree of price discovery among both buyers and sellers. Will there be a surge in new listings? Will pent-up demand accelerate the market forward? If there is no vaccine on the horizon and remote work continues to be the norm, will the exodus to the suburbs be a lasting trend? Will NYC schools reopen this fall? The post-lockdown supply/demand dynamic will likely provide insight into the future direction of NYC home values and market pricing.
 
This unprecedented and challenging period continues to have a significant impact on our daily lives and on how we live and view the importance of our homes.

Compass Market Watch: Market Metrics
Per Compass Market Watch, contracts signed/new listing inventory declined from 54% to 26% over the three month period from March 1 to May 31. (New York City's lockdown commenced on March 22 and lasted for eight weeks). Following a similar pattern during the month of June, for the 7 days prior to June 22, contracts signed/new listings equated to 24%.
 
Hopefully, we see a reverse in this downward trend with the reopening of NYC in Phase 2 and the restart of in-person showings. The current market metrics imply an increase in supply and weakened demand: an opportunity for buyers and greater negotiability on sale price. In addition to price discounts, historically low mortgage rates are likely to drive millennial buyers back into the market (especially in the under-$2 million affordable luxury segment). It may take time for the luxury market ($4 million to $10 million-plus) to recover, as many of these buyers have left the city and are not likely to return until after Labor Day.

Marketing Your Home for Sale in the Post-Lockdown Era
The coronavirus pandemic has forever changed how we will market and sell homes. The traditional open house is history as co-op and condo boards will restrict non-resident traffic, and sellers prefer not to have strangers walk through their homes en masse. As a sales broker using virtual tools, I can devote more time to vetting and qualifying buyers (with limited purposeful in-person showings) and focus on targeted outreach to sell your residence or investment property.
 
The online experience will now play a significant role in identifying a potential, serious buyer. In addition to professional photography and floor plan drawings, we are experiencing a seismic shift in the use of video, 3D renderings, virtual tours and email brochures to market a listing and communicate with buyers. As a seller, your "first showing" is now online versus an in-person tour of your home. The digital presentation of a for-sale listing is critical. Your residence needs to stand out against the competition to garner interest and increase the likelihood of a successful outcome (pricing + presentation = success).
 
The buyer experience of the future will entail an in-depth review of many listings online using virtual tools and then working with a broker/advisor to narrow those selections to three or four properties for in-person inspections before submitting offers.
